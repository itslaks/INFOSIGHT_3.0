üìñ 3. Understanding the Basics

What is DNS?

Simple Explanation: DNS (Domain Name System) is like the phonebook of the internet. Just like you use a phonebook to find a phone number using a person's name, DNS helps your computer find a website's location using its domain name.

Example: When you type "google.com", DNS translates it to an IP address like "142.250.80.46" so your computer knows where to find Google's servers.

Types of DNS Records:

  • A Record: Points your domain to an IPv4 address (like 192.168.1.1)
  • AAAA Record: Points to an IPv6 address (newer, longer format)
  • MX Record: Tells where your email should be delivered
  • TXT Record: Stores text information, often used for security
  • NS Record: Shows which servers manage your domain

What is SSL/TLS?

Simple Explanation: SSL/TLS is like a secure envelope for data traveling between your computer and a website. It encrypts (scrambles) the information so hackers can't read it.

Real-World Example: When you see a padlock üîí in your browser's address bar, that means SSL/TLS is protecting your connection. Without it, anyone could see your passwords, credit card numbers, or personal messages.

What are Security Headers?

Simple Explanation: Security headers are like instructions that a website gives to your browser, telling it how to handle and display the website safely.

Analogy: Think of them as warning labels on products. Just as "Keep out of reach of children" protects kids, security headers protect users from online threats.

What is Email Security (SPF, DKIM, DMARC)?

Simple Explanation: These are like security stamps that prove an email is really from who it claims to be, not from a scammer pretending to be someone else.

SPF: Makes a list of approved email servers that can send mail for your domain
DKIM: Adds a digital signature to prove the email wasn't tampered with
DMARC: Sets rules for what to do with suspicious emails

What are Ports?

Simple Explanation: Ports are like numbered doors on a building. Each door (port) is used for a specific service. Some doors should be locked (closed ports), while others need to be open for services to work.

Examples:
‚Ä¢ Port 80 = HTTP (regular websites)
‚Ä¢ Port 443 = HTTPS (secure websites)
‚Ä¢ Port 22 = SSH (remote server access)
‚Ä¢ Port 25 = SMTP (sending email)

SSL/TLS Certificate Analysis

What we check:

  • Certificate Validity: Is the certificate expired or valid?
  • Expiration Date: When will the certificate expire?
  • Issuer: Who issued the certificate? (e.g., Let's Encrypt, DigiCert)
  • Encryption Strength: How strong is the encryption? (128-bit, 256-bit)
  • Protocol Version: Which security protocol is used? (TLS 1.2, TLS 1.3)
  • Subject Alternative Names (SAN): Which domains are covered?
‚ö†Ô∏è Common Issues:
  • Expired certificates make browsers show "Not Secure" warnings
  • Self-signed certificates aren't trusted by browsers
  • Weak encryption can be broken by attackers
  • Old protocols (SSLv3, TLS 1.0) have known vulnerabilities

Security Headers

Critical Headers:

Strict-Transport-Security (HSTS)

What it does: Forces browsers to only use HTTPS connections
Why it matters: Prevents attackers from downgrading your connection to insecure HTTP
Example value: max-age=31536000; includeSubDomains; preload
Content-Security-Policy (CSP)

What it does: Controls what content can be loaded on your webpage
Why it matters: Prevents XSS attacks, clickjacking, and code injection
Real-world impact: Stops hackers from injecting malicious scripts into your pages

Important Headers:

X-Frame-Options

What it does: Prevents your site from being embedded in frames/iframes
Why it matters: Protects against clickjacking attacks where attackers trick users into clicking hidden elements
Recommended value: DENY or SAMEORIGIN
X-Content-Type-Options

What it does: Prevents browsers from guessing file types
Why it matters: Stops MIME-sniffing attacks where malicious files are disguised as safe files
Required value: nosniff

Email Security Configuration

SPF (Sender Policy Framework)

Purpose: Lists which mail servers are allowed to send email for your domain

Example Record: v=spf1 include:_spf.google.com ~all

How it protects you:
  • Prevents spammers from sending fake emails pretending to be from your domain
  • Reduces the chance your emails are marked as spam
  • Protects your brand reputation

DMARC (Domain-based Message Authentication)

Purpose: Tells email providers what to do with emails that fail authentication

Policy Options:
  • none: Just monitor, don't block anything
  • quarantine: Send suspicious emails to spam folder
  • reject: Block suspicious emails completely

Best Practice: Use "reject" policy for maximum protection

DKIM (DomainKeys Identified Mail)

Purpose: Adds a digital signature to your emails to prove they're authentic

How it works: Like a wax seal on a letter - if the seal is broken, you know someone tampered with it

Protection: Prevents email tampering and proves your emails are genuine

Common Ports and Their Risk Levels:

Step-by-Step Fix Guides

How to Fix SSL/TLS Issues

Step 1: Get a Free SSL Certificate ‚Ä¢ Use Let's Encrypt (free, automated, trusted) ‚Ä¢ Visit: https://letsencrypt.org/ ‚Ä¢ Most web hosts offer one-click SSL installation

Step 2: Install the Certificate ‚Ä¢ Contact your hosting provider, or ‚Ä¢ Use cPanel/Plesk SSL/TLS section, or ‚Ä¢ Use Certbot (automated tool)

Step 3: Force HTTPS ‚Ä¢ Redirect all HTTP traffic to HTTPS ‚Ä¢ Add this to your .htaccess file: RewriteEngine On RewriteCond %{HTTPS} off RewriteRule ^(.*)$ https://%{HTTP_HOST}%{REQUEST_URI} [L,R=301]

Step 4: Set Auto-Renewal ‚Ä¢ Let's Encrypt certificates expire every 90 days ‚Ä¢ Set up automatic renewal to avoid downtime

How to Add Security Headers

For Apache (.htaccess file): # Add security headers Header set Strict-Transport-Security "max-age=31536000; includeSubDomains; preload" Header set X-Frame-Options "DENY" Header set X-Content-Type-Options "nosniff" Header set X-XSS-Protection "1; mode=block" Header set Referrer-Policy "strict-origin-when-cross-origin" Header set Content-Security-Policy "default-src 'self'"

For Nginx (nginx.conf): add_header Strict-Transport-Security "max-age=31536000; includeSubDomains; preload" always; add_header X-Frame-Options "DENY" always; add_header X-Content-Type-Options "nosniff" always; add_header X-XSS-Protection "1; mode=block" always; add_header Referrer-Policy "strict-origin-when-cross-origin" always; add_header Content-Security-Policy "default-src 'self'" always;

Note: Test thoroughly after adding headers, as CSP can break site functionality if too strict

How to Configure Email Security

Step 1: Add SPF Record ‚Ä¢ Log into your DNS provider (GoDaddy, Cloudflare, etc.) ‚Ä¢ Add a TXT record for your domain ‚Ä¢ Record name: @ (or your domain) ‚Ä¢ Record value: v=spf1 include:_spf.google.com ~all (Replace with your email provider's SPF record)

Step 2: Add DMARC Record ‚Ä¢ Add another TXT record ‚Ä¢ Record name: _dmarc ‚Ä¢ Record value: v=DMARC1; p=quarantine; rua=mailto:your@email.com

Step 3: Set up DKIM ‚Ä¢ Contact your email provider for DKIM keys ‚Ä¢ Add the provided TXT record to your DNS ‚Ä¢ Usually looks like: selector._domainkey

Step 4: Test and Monitor ‚Ä¢ Wait 24-48 hours for DNS propagation ‚Ä¢ Re-scan with Site Index to verify ‚Ä¢ Gradually change DMARC policy from 'none' ‚Üí 'quarantine' ‚Üí 'reject'

How to Secure Open Ports

Method 1: Close Unnecessary Ports
  • Use your firewall to block unused ports
  • Contact your hosting provider to close dangerous ports
  • Only keep ports open that your services need

Method 2: Restrict Access by IP
  • If you must keep sensitive ports open (SSH, MySQL)
  • Whitelist only your IP addresses
  • Use VPN for remote access

Method 3: Use Strong Authentication
  • Disable password authentication for SSH
  • Use SSH keys instead
  • Enable two-factor authentication where available

‚ùì 8. Frequently Asked Questions

General Questions

Q: Is this tool safe to use?

A: Yes, absolutely. Site Index only performs passive scanning - it reads publicly available information about your website. It doesn't attempt to exploit vulnerabilities or harm your site in any way. Think of it like checking if your front door is locked without actually trying to break in.

Q: How often should I scan my website?

A: We recommend:
  • After major changes: Every time you update your server or security settings
  • Regular checkups: Once per month for most sites
  • High-security sites: Weekly or after any configuration change
  • After security news: When new vulnerabilities are announced

Q: Can I scan any website?

A: Legally, you should only scan:
  • Websites you own
  • Websites you manage (with client permission)
  • Websites where you have explicit written permission
Scanning websites without permission may violate terms of service or laws in your jurisdiction.

Q: Why is my scan taking so long?

A: Scans typically take 10-30 seconds. Longer scans may occur due to:
  • Slow DNS responses
  • Server rate limiting
  • Network congestion
  • Firewalls blocking certain checks
If a scan exceeds 60 seconds, try again later or check if the domain is accessible.

Score and Results Questions

Q: What's a good security score?

A:
  • 8.0+: Excellent - Well protected
  • 6.0-7.9: Good - Some improvements needed
  • 4.0-5.9: Fair - Significant work needed
  • Below 4.0: Poor/Critical - Immediate action required
For most websites, aim for 7.5 or higher.

Q: My score is low. Does this mean I'll definitely get hacked?

A: Not necessarily, but you're at higher risk. A low score means you're missing important security protections. Think of it like leaving your house unlocked - you might not get robbed, but you're making it much easier for thieves. Fix the issues to reduce your risk significantly.

Q: I got a perfect 10/10 score. Am I completely secure?

A: While a 10/10 is excellent, no website is 100% secure. This scan checks many important security measures, but it can't test everything (like your code quality, database security, or user password strength). A perfect score means you have strong foundational security, but you should still:
  • Keep software updated
  • Use strong passwords
  • Monitor for suspicious activity
  • Regular backups
  • Stay informed about new threats

Q: Why do different security tools give different scores?

A: Different tools weigh factors differently and test different aspects of security. Some focus heavily on SSL, others on headers, etc. Use multiple tools for a comprehensive view. Our scoring balances all major security aspects equally.

Technical Questions

Q: What does "SSL certificate expires in X days" mean?

A: SSL certificates have an expiration date (usually 90 days for free certificates, 1 year for paid). When a certificate expires, browsers will show scary warning messages to your visitors. You need to renew your certificate before it expires. Most modern systems can do this automatically.

Q: What is "Content-Security-Policy" and why is it important?

A: CSP is like a security guard for your website. It tells browsers which resources (scripts, images, etc.) are allowed to load on your pages. This prevents attackers from injecting malicious code. Without CSP, if someone finds a way to add code to your site, browsers will run it without question.

Q: I have SPF but no DMARC. Is that okay?

A: It's better than nothing, but not ideal. Think of it this way:
  • SPF alone: Like having a list of approved visitors but no security guard
  • SPF + DMARC: Having the list AND a security guard who checks it and takes action
  • All three (SPF + DMARC + DKIM): Multiple layers of verification
You should implement all three for proper email security.

Q: Why does my site show open ports?

A: Ports are necessary for services to work:
  • Ports 80/443 (HTTP/HTTPS): Normal and necessary for websites
  • Port 22 (SSH): May be needed for server management (secure it properly)
  • Ports 21, 23, 3389: Should usually be closed (use secure alternatives)
The issue isn't having open ports - it's having unnecessary or improperly secured ones.

Troubleshooting

Q: The scan failed with an error. What should I do?

A: Common solutions:
  • Check that you entered the domain correctly (no http://, no trailing slashes)
  • Verify the domain is accessible from your browser
  • Check if the domain has DNS records configured
  • Some very restrictive firewalls may block scanning tools
  • Try again in a few minutes - temporary network issues

Q: Why can't the scanner access my site?

A: Possible reasons:
  • Domain not propagated: New domains take 24-48 hours to work globally
  • Firewall blocking: Your server firewall may block security scanners
  • Cloudflare protection: May require temporarily disabling "I'm Under Attack" mode
  • Development site: Sites behind authentication or not publicly accessible

Q: My hosting provider says their security is good, but I got a low score. Why?

A: Hosting providers secure their infrastructure, but YOU must secure your website configuration. It's like living in a gated community - the neighborhood is secure, but you still need to lock your own doors. Security headers, SSL configuration, and email security are typically your responsibility to configure.

Privacy and Data

Q: Do you store my scan results?

A: Scan results are generated in real-time and not permanently stored on our servers. The data exists only during your browser session. Once you close the page, the results are gone.

Q: Can other people see my scan results?

A: No. Each scan is private and only visible to you during your session. We don't create public databases or share results.

Q: Is the information you show publicly available?

A: Yes. Everything this tool checks is publicly accessible information:
  • SSL certificates are public by design
  • HTTP headers are sent with every page request
  • DNS records are publicly queryable
  • WHOIS data is public domain registration info
  • Open ports can be detected by anyone
We're not accessing private data - we're just organizing public information in a helpful way.
